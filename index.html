Published: 1887

A Study in Scarlet

The story marks the first appearance of Sherlock Holmes and Dr. Watson, who would become the most famous detective duo in literature. The book's title derives from a speech given by Holmes, a consulting detective, to his friend and chronicler Watson on the nature of his work, in which he describes the story's murder investigation as his "study in scarlet": "There's the scarlet thread of murder running through the colourless skein of life, and our duty is to unravel it, and isolate it, and expose every inch of it."

Part 1: After returning from the Second Anglo-Afghan War, Dr. John Watson moves in with Sherlock Holmes at 221B Baker Street. Holmes, a consulting detective, impresses Watson with his deductive prowess. They investigate the murder of Enoch Drebber, finding a cryptic message ("RACHE") and a wedding ring. Holmes deduces the murderer's description and sets a trap with a duplicate ring. A suspect, Hope, is arrested but later revealed to be the murderer when Stangerson, another victim, is found with the same message. Using street urchins, Holmes captures Hope, the cabby, who confesses to the murders.

Part 2: In 1847 Utah, John Ferrier and Lucy, saved by Mormons, face a choice: convert or die. In 1860, Lucy loves Jefferson Hope but is forced to marry. Lucy dies heartbroken, fueling Hope's quest for vengeance. Across continents and years, Hope hunts down his targets, culminating in a gripping showdown in London. As Hope exacts chilling revenge, readers are drawn into a tale of love, betrayal, and justice, where hope and despair collide in a riveting narrative. Holmes deduced the case and Watson decides to write about it.

Published: February, 1892

The Speckled Band

"The Speckled Band" is a classic locked-room mystery that deals with the themes of parental greed, inheritance and freedom. Tinged with Gothic elements, it is considered by many to be one of Doyle's finest works, with the author himself calling it his best story.

Sherlock Holmes and Dr. Watson embark on a gripping adventure at Stoke Moran, where danger lurks in the shadows of a sinister stepfather's schemes. With Helen Stoner's life on the line, they uncover a tangled web of deceit spun by Dr. Grimesby Roylott, a menacing figure with a troubled past. As they race against time, Holmes confronts a venomous foe, revealing Roylott's chilling plot to claim his stepdaughter's fortune. In a thrilling showdown, justice is served, but not without sacrifice, as Holmes navigates a moral dilemma to shield Helen from further heartache.

Published: February 1893

The Adventure of the Yellow Face

One of Doyle's sentimental pieces, the story is remarkable in that Holmes' deduction during the course of it proves incorrect. According to Dr. Watson:

"...where he failed it happened too often that no one else succeeded... Now and again, however, it chanced that even when he erred the truth was still discovered."

Holmes deduces from a visitor's forgotten pipe that the man is disturbed but muscular, left-handed, and careless. When Grant Munro seeks Holmes's help over his wife's suspicious behavior, Holmes suspects her first husband is blackmailing her. Investigating, they find a masked figure in a cottage, who turns out to be Effie's daughter from her first marriage. Effie confesses her secret, and Munro forgives her, embracing their daughter. Touched by Munro's response, Holmes reflects on the case, promising to remain vigilant.

Published: September 1893

The Adventure of the Greek Interpreter

The Adventure of the Greek Interpreter", one of the 56 Sherlock Holmes short stories written by Sir Arthur Conan Doyle, is one of 12 stories in the cycle collected as The Memoirs of Sherlock Holmes. The story was originally published in The Strand Magazine (UK) and Harper's Weekly (US) in September 1893.[1] This story introduces Holmes's elder brother Mycroft. Doyle ranked "The Greek Interpreter" seventeenth in a list of his nineteen favourite Sherlock Holmes stories.

One summer evening, Dr. Watson learns of Sherlock Holmes's elder brother, Mycroft, whose powers of deduction rival Holmes's own. Despite his brilliance, Mycroft lacks the ambition for detective work. When a Greek interpreter named Melas recounts a harrowing ordeal involving a kidnapping, Mycroft seeks Holmes's help. Melas describes being coerced into translating for a captive, Kratides, who refuses to sign over property to his captors. Holmes and Mycroft investigate, leading them to a house in Beckenham where Melas and Kratides are found bound and in danger. Though Melas survives, Kratides is dead. It's revealed that Kratides was Sophy's brother, seeking to protect her from her captors. The perpetrators meet their demise in Hungary, likely at Sophy's hands, avenging her brother's abuse.

Published: December 1893

The Adventure of the Final Problem

The story, set in 1891, introduces the criminal mastermind Professor Moriarty. It was intended to be the final Holmes story, ending with the character's death, but Conan Doyle was later persuaded to revive Holmes for additional stories and novels.

Holmes, wounded from escaping Moriarty's gang, confides in Watson about his pursuit of the criminal organization. Fearing Moriarty's intellect, Holmes plans to flee to Europe while the police capture Moriarty. Watson joins him, and they evade Moriarty's pursuit across Europe. At Reichenbach Falls, Holmes sacrifices himself in a final showdown with Moriarty to protect Watson.Watson discovers the tragic end of their adventure, with Holmes and Moriarty both lost in the swirling waters of the gorge. Watson returns to England, mourning Holmes's death but grateful for his bravery. The Moriarty gang is brought to justice, thanks to Holmes's efforts. Reflecting on Holmes's brilliance, Watson concludes that Sherlock Holmes was truly the greatest man he had ever known.

Published: March 25, 1902

The Hound of the Baskervilles

Doyle's "The Hound of the Baskervilles," largely in Dartmoor, Devon in England's West Country and follows Holmes and Watson investigating the legend of a fearsome, diabolical hound of supernatural origin. In writing the novel, Doyle drew inspiration from the local legend of Sir Richard Cabell of Brook Hall in Buckfastleigh, Devon, who was rumored to have sold his soul to the devil and, upon his burial, a ghostly pack of hounds were said to have come across the moors to howl at his grave. Holmes returns after his supposed demise, leading to the novel's acclaim. Voted among the UK's top novels, it's hailed by Sherlockians as the best Holmes tale.

Sherlock Holmes deciphers a cryptic warning, leading to a murder investigation at Birlstone House. With Inspector MacDonald, they uncover a tangled web of intrigue surrounding the victim, Douglas. As secrets unravel, they confront a sinister plot involving a mysterious American and a chilling past in the Valley of Fear. In a thrilling twist, Douglas reveals his true identity as Birdy Edwards, a former Pinkerton detective entangled in a deadly game of survival. With Moriarty's shadow looming, Holmes vows to unravel the truth and bring justice to those who seek to silence the innocent.

Published: 1910

The Adventure of the Devil's Foot

"The Adventure of the Devil's Foot" from 1910 is one of the 56 Sherlock Holmes short stories written by Arthur Conan Doyle.

Holmes and Watson are summoned to Cornwall by the local vicar and Mortimer Tregennis after a mysterious death. Tregennis's siblings are found dead and insane after a night of cards. The next day, Mortimer also dies in the same manner. Holmes deduces a poisonous plant activated by fire as the murder weapon. He reveals Mortimer as the culprit of the initial crime and accuses Dr. Sterndale, a family friend, of Mortimer's murder. Sterndale confesses, motivated by unrequited love for Brenda Tregennis. Holmes advises Sterndale to leave England and return to Africa.

Published: 1913

The Adventure of the Dying Detective

"The Adventure of the Dying Detective", in some editions simply titled "The Dying Detective", is one of the 56 Sherlock Holmes short stories that were written by Sir Arthur Conan Doyle. It was originally published in Collier's in the United States on 22 November 1913, and The Strand Magazine in the United Kingdom in December 1913.[1] Together with seven other stories, it was collected in His Last Bow (published 1917).

Dr. Watson rushes to tend to Sherlock Holmes, who appears to be dying from a rare tropical disease. Holmes, refusing Watson's help, insults his abilities and demands that Watson wait before seeking medical assistance. Holmes instructs Watson to bring Mr. Culverton Smith to him but insists that Watson leave before Smith arrives.
Watson convinces Smith to visit Holmes, where Smith confesses to poisoning Holmes with the same illness that killed his nephew. As Smith tries to conceal evidence of his crime, Holmes signals Inspector Morton to enter. Holmes reveals his feigned illness as a ploy to induce Smith's confession. With Watson as a witness, Smith is arrested for murder. Holmes clarifies that he respected Watson's medical expertise but needed to maintain the ruse to trap Smith.

Published: September 1917

His Last Bow (Short Story)

One of 56 short stories about Sherlock Holmes written by Sir Arthur Conan Doyle.The narration is in the third person, instead of the first person narration usually provided by the character of Dr. Watson, and it is a spy story, rather than a detective mystery. Due to its portrayal of British and German spies on the eve of war, its publication during the First World War, and its patriotic themes, the story has been interpreted as a propaganda tool intended to boost morale for British readers.

As World War I looms, German agent Von Bork prepares to flee England with his trove of intelligence. His contact, "Altamont," arrives, but it's actually Sherlock Holmes in disguise. Holmes and Watson, posing as the chauffeur, apprehend Von Bork after revealing they've been feeding false information to the Germans. Holmes, retired but called back for this case, has meticulously tracked down spies across continents. With Von Bork captured, Holmes and Watson deliver him to Scotland Yard. Reflecting on the war's approach, Holmes cryptically tells Watson of an "east wind" coming, symbolizing the impending upheaval.

Published: 1927

The Adventure of the Veiled Lodger

"The Adventure of the Veiled Lodger" (1927), one of the 56 Sherlock Holmes short stories written by British author Sir Arthur Conan Doyle, is one of 12 stories in the cycle collected as The Case-Book of Sherlock Holmes.

Mrs. Merrilow seeks Holmes's help with her lodger, Mrs. Ronder, whose disfigured face and nighttime outbursts suggest a troubling past. Referencing Abbas Parva, Holmes recalls a tragic circus incident involving Mr. Ronder's death. Mrs. Ronder confesses to a plan to kill her abusive husband with her lover, Leonardo, who then abandoned her. Despite contemplating suicide, Mrs. Ronder finds hope in Holmes's words. Her decision not to end her life marks a new beginning.

Did you know?

Sherrinford Holmes?

Sir Arthur Conan Doyle originally named the detective Sherrinford but changed it, possibly due to the existence of Nottingham cricketers named Sherwin and Shacklock, as the author was a cricket enthusiast.

Real life inspiration

Doyle was inspired by
Dr. Joseph Bell, a notable Scottish surgeon known for his diagnostic prowess and astute observation, in crafting the character of Sherlock Holmes.

Honorary Fellowship

In 2002, Britain's Royal Society of Chemistry honored the detective for his use of forensic science and analytical chemistry to solve crimes, making Sherlock Holmes the first and only fictional character to be awarded an honorary fellowship.

Language is no barrier

Doyle's books and stories have been translated into 98 languages
